[MYSTIKS REVIEW] Abadi Nan Jaya, Belaban Hidup, Day Zero — A New Era for Southeast Asian Horror Fiction?

By Risk · Date 9 Jun 2026 · Category Review
🌐 Baca dalam Bahasa Malaysia BM

Horror, since the stone age (judging by the markings on cave walls left by people long gone) has never been a stranger to us. From predators capable of tearing flesh from bone to diseases that kill without warning, horror occurs when our lives feel threatened.

The horror we want to discuss right now is the kind born from the mind of George Romero: the Zombie. The living dead. After the early 2000s boom in zombie horror — driven by AMC's The Walking Dead series and Danny Boyle's 28 Days Later — Malaysia jumped on the bandwagon too. Director Mamat Khalid created Zombi Kampung Pisang, a horror comedy. But behind an industry increasingly hungry for new ideas, most Malaysian zombie films have remained trapped in the shadow of the late Mamat Khalid.

It's not that Malaysia has never made a good zombie story — it has — but the industry here has remained too tightly veiled. Antidot on TV3, the drama series billed as Malaysia's answer to The Walking Dead, stands as perhaps the most textbook definition of Missed Potential the industry has ever produced.

Even so, Ray Lee's 2021 film Belaban Hidup became something of a lifeline for an industry increasingly dominated by neighbouring countries. The story — set in an Iban village on the island of Borneo — brought a different atmosphere and a more serious narrative. Even five years after its release, and despite a visual quality that sometimes resembles footage shot on an old cassette handycam, we at Mystiks believe the Malaysian film industry could and should draw inspiration from this homegrown film.

Fun fact: the film won international awards.

The Asian Film Industry After Train to Busan

Train to Busan — even a passing stranger would know that title, no introduction needed. It's the South Korean zombie film loved by virtually everyone. Why did it succeed in the eyes of global critics? We'll get to that in a moment.

In the wake of Train to Busan, many films have tried to recapture its magic, and some have succeeded. Among them: Outside (2021), Day Zero (2024), and Abadi Nan Jaya (The Elixir) (2025). What made them work?

The conclusion we have reached from these four films is that they all succeed in making us, as viewers, lose hope. This is especially true of Abadi Nan Jaya (2025). Horror lands harder when we as viewers understand why something is frightening. Sure, you could say, “It's a zombie — it eats people, that's scary enough.” But Malaysian zombie films almost always end with a Happy Ending. Zombi Kampung Pisang: RELA (the civil defence corps) finds a cure. KL Zombi: an MLM product becomes the cure. Antidot on TV3: the cure is in the main character's child's blood.

Hope inside a zombie film is what kills hope for the entire industry.

Malaysia needs a zombie film that is genuinely, unflinchingly bleak — one whose ending sends you out of the cinema feeling sad, or angry.

If every film wraps up like a Disney musical, how are we ever going to advance in this genre?

If our neighbouring countries can hold and master this genre — when is it our turn?
